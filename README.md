# 🇳🇵 Nepal Jobs Visualizer (AI Exposure Explorer)

An interactive, "vibe-coded" research tool that maps the Nepalese labor market against the rising tide of AI automation. Inspired by [Andrej Karpathy's US Job Market Visualizer](https://karpathy.ai/jobs/).

## 🚀 Overview

This tool provides a data-driven look at 52 major occupations in Nepal. It helps you explore how AI might reshape the Nepalese economy, contrasting high-risk urban roles with the resilient, physical labor that forms the backbone of the country.

### Key Features:
- **52 Occupations:** From subsistence farmers to IT professionals.
- **AI Exposure Scores:** Rated 0–10 based on task-level LLM automation potential in a local context.
- **Monthly Salaries:** Real NPR benchmarks based on national projections.
- **Workforce Distribution:** Visualizing the scale of each sector (Agriculture, Services, IT, etc.).
- **Standalone UI:** Zero dependencies. Works offline by opening a single HTML file.
- **Brilliant Color Scale:** High-contrast color coding for AI risk (Green → Amber → Red).

---

## 📊 Data Sources

The data is synthesized from official national sources:
- **Workforce Size:** Nepal National Census 2021.
- **Salaries:** Nepal Labour Force Survey (NLFS) 2024/2025 projections.
- **Job Classification:** National Standard Classification of Occupations (NSCO) 2021.
- **AI Exposure Analysis:** Scored specifically for the Nepalese context (accounting for digital infrastructure, informal sectors, and physical terrain).

## 🛠️ How to Run Locally

You don't need a server or any complex setup.

1. Clone or download this repository.
2. Navigate to the `nepal/site/` folder.
3. Open `index.html` in any modern web browser.

---

## 💡 Why built this?

Most AI exposure research focuses on the Western world. In Nepal, the story is different. While our large agricultural and manual labor sectors have a very low AI exposure score (~2.3/10 avg), our fastest-growing urban professions (Accounting, Clerical work, Support services) are on the front lines of the AI shift. 

This tool is meant for students, policy-makers, and workers to visualize that gap.
